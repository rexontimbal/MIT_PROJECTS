AGNES

Agglomerative Nesting for Accident Hotspot Detection

A data-driven road accident hotspot detection system built for the Philippine National Police — Caraga Region (PRO-13). AGNES identifies high-risk road segments through spatial clustering of geocoded accident records, enabling targeted road safety interventions.

What is the AGNES Hotspot Detection System?

The AGNES Hotspot Detection System is a web-based geospatial platform designed to assist the Philippine National Police (PNP) Caraga Region in identifying, analyzing, and responding to road accident hotspots across the region's provinces: Agusan del Norte, Agusan del Sur, Surigao del Norte, Surigao del Sur, and Dinagat Islands.

By collecting and geocoding accident reports from police stations throughout the region, the system applies the AGNES (Agglomerative Nesting) hierarchical clustering algorithm to group nearby accidents into spatial clusters. These clusters are then ranked by severity, frequency, and casualty count to reveal which road segments pose the greatest risk to public safety.

The system transforms raw accident data into actionable intelligence, enabling PNP leadership and local government units to prioritize road safety interventions where they are needed most.

The AGNES Algorithm

AGNES (Agglomerative Nesting) is a bottom-up hierarchical clustering method widely used in spatial data analysis. Unlike partitioning methods such as K-Means, AGNES does not require the number of clusters to be specified in advance — making it ideal for accident hotspot detection where the number and size of clusters are unknown.

The algorithm uses geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude) of accident locations and a configurable distance threshold to determine which accidents are spatially related. It produces a hierarchy of clusters (dendrogram) that can be cut at different levels to reveal hotspots of varying granularity.

1

Geocode Accidents

Each accident report is geocoded using its recorded address, producing a latitude/longitude coordinate for spatial analysis.

2

Compute Distances

The Haversine formula calculates the great-circle distance between every pair of accident points, accounting for Earth's curvature.

3

Merge Nearest Pairs

The two closest clusters are iteratively merged based on the selected linkage method (Ward, Complete, Average, or Single) until no more merges are possible within the distance threshold.

4

Form Hotspots

Resulting clusters that meet the minimum size requirement are identified as accident hotspots. Each hotspot is ranked by total accidents, fatalities, and severity score.

Accident Report Management

Multi-level report submission, review, and approval workflow aligned with the PNP chain of command from station to regional level.

Analytics Dashboard

Real-time statistics on accident trends, severity breakdown, monthly patterns, top provinces, and clustering job results.

Adjustable distance threshold, minimum cluster size, and linkage method to fine-tune hotspot detection for different analysis needs.

Data-driven road safety intervention recommendations for each hotspot, formatted for submission to the Department of Public Works and Highways.

Role-Based Access Control

Hierarchical user roles from Traffic Officer to Regional Director, ensuring data security and proper chain-of-command workflows.

Who Uses This System?

AGNES serves the PNP Caraga Region's road safety mission through a role-based hierarchy:

Regional Director Oversees all regional accident data, approves reports, and directs safety interventions across PRO-13.
Provincial Chief Reviews and approves accident reports for the province and coordinates with LGUs on hotspot areas.
Station Commander Manages station-level reports and monitors local accident patterns within the station's jurisdiction.
Traffic Officer Records accident details at the scene and submits reports for review through the chain of command.
Data Encoder Digitizes paper-based accident records (ACIR/INQUEST forms) into the system for spatial analysis.

System Objectives

The system was developed to address the following objectives:

  • Automate the identification of accident hotspots using spatial clustering
  • Provide a centralized, digital accident reporting workflow for PNP Caraga
  • Enable evidence-based road safety planning and resource allocation
  • Generate data-driven recommendations aligned with DPWH safety standards
  • Support the Philippine Road Safety Action Plan through localized data analysis

How Hotspots Are Determined

A hotspot is a geographic cluster of road accidents that indicates a persistently dangerous road segment or intersection. The system determines hotspots through the following criteria:

Spatial Proximity

Accidents within the configured distance threshold (e.g., 500 meters) are grouped together. Closer accidents have a stronger association.

Minimum Cluster Size

Only clusters meeting the minimum number of accidents (e.g., 3 or more) are classified as hotspots, filtering out isolated incidents.

Severity Scoring

Hotspots are ranked using a weighted severity score: fatalities carry the highest weight, followed by injuries, then property-damage-only incidents.

Risk Classification

Each hotspot is classified as Critical, High, Medium, or Low risk based on its severity score, guiding the urgency of intervention.
